Designing quarterly progress reports that actually align to licensure standards

Duccio Mondanelli · 2026 · ~8 min read

Every teacher apprenticeship program has a quarterly progress report. Almost none of them can answer the only question the report exists to answer: is this person on track to become the specific kind of teacher we are licensing them to be?

Ours couldn't either, at first. This is the story of how I rebuilt it, told so that you can skip the mistakes.

The problem: one generic form for two different licenses

Our program prepares special education teachers in two Minnesota licensure tracks: Academic and Behavioral Strategist (ABS), which is K-12, and Early Childhood Special Education (ECSE), which runs birth to age six. These are significantly different jobs. An ECSE teacher writes IFSPs, coaches families in natural environments, and works under Part C requirements for infants and toddlers. An ABS teacher runs FBAs and BIPs, designs academic interventions, and manages K-12 IEP and eligibility processes.

Our original progress report treated them identically. The content standards section used four vague categories like "foundational knowledge" that mapped to no actual standard in either license. The form never distinguished an IFSP from an IEP. It had no notion of what a candidate should be able to do by quarter two versus quarter four. Mentors filled it out because they had to, and the data told us almost nothing.

A progress report that doesn't map to the licensure standards isn't measuring progress. It's checking a box, assuming compliance with the act of filling out a form.

The redesign, in four moves

1. Split the form

The first decision was the hardest to accept: one form for two programs was the original problem, and no amount of clever section design would fix it. We built two separate instruments, visually distinct at a glance so nobody ever grabs the wrong one. Colors are used to create clarity, implementing core ideas of intentional design in a space often constrained by academic and insitutional bindings.

2. Map every item to a real standard

Every content item on each form now traces to something a licensing body or the program itself actually requires: Minnesota's PELSB standards for each license, the program's core skills, and High Leverage Practices. If an item couldn't be traced, it got cut. This sounds obvious. It required going line by line through the standards for both licenses and being willing to delete sections people were used to.

One rule from that process worth stealing: use only the frameworks your program has actually adopted. Our first redesign draft included a well-regarded national framework that we don't use, because it seemed like it belonged. It didn't. Borrowed rigor is fake rigor.

3. Build in quarter-based progression

A form that asks the same questions in quarter one and quarter seven is telling candidates that nothing is supposed to change. We wrote explicit expectations by phase, from observation through co-teaching to independent practice, so a "developing" rating in an early quarter reads as normal and the same rating in a late quarter reads as a flag. The form now carries the program's theory of growth inside it.

4. Design for the mentor's thirty seconds

The person completing this form is a mentor teacher with a full caseload. Every design decision was optimized for their time: dropdown selections instead of free text wherever a rating is structured, visual grouping that matches how an observation actually flows, and nothing that requires them to go look something up. We built the documents programmatically, which meant we could also do things standard templates can't, like styling dropdowns so they're impossible to miss. The tooling details matter less than the principle: if the busiest person in the loop can't complete the instrument accurately in one sitting, the instrument is wrong.

What changed

We now have actual data on progress. Where we previously struggled to identify candidate readiness, we now have strong, specific evidence to support placement and intervention decisions. The results speak for themselves: candidates are on track for their year-long student teaching; mentors report high satisfaction in the data-reporting process; the program is progressing smoothly.

What I'd tell another program

  • If you serve multiple licensure areas, you need multiple instruments. Accept it early.
  • Trace every item to a standard or cut it. The deletion is the redesign.
  • Encode progression. The form should know what quarter it is.
  • Optimize for the mentor, not the program office. The program office reads the data; the mentor creates it.
  • Whoever on your team can script document generation is worth their weight in gold. Hand-maintaining parallel Word templates is how alignment quietly rots.
